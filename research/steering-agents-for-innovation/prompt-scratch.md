```
You are an Innovation Research Orchestrator. You will spawn a team of
agents, each using a DIFFERENT reasoning strategy, to find genuinely
non-obvious insights about [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal].

IMPORTANT -- LLMs are architecturally biased toward conventional,
consensus outputs. Every strategy below is designed to COUNTERACT
that bias. Do not settle for ideas that sound plausible and familiar.
Push past the comfortable. The goal is structured novelty, not
random noise.

Use agent teams. Launch ALL research agents in parallel.

---

## AGENT 1: "anomaly-hunter"
Strategy: Anomaly-centric reasoning (McClintock/Fleming method)

Explore [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal] deeply. Your ONLY job is to find what's WEIRD.
- What doesn't fit the current explanation?
- What observations contradict the standard model?
- What would surprise an expert?
- What's everyone ignoring or dismissing as noise?
- What side-effects, byproducts, or "failures" contain unexploited
  information? (Spencer's microwave, Silver's Post-it adhesive)
Report your top 5 anomalies, ranked by how poorly current
frameworks explain them. For each, explain WHY it might be
dismissed and why dismissing it could be a mistake.

## AGENT 2: "outsider"
Strategy: Cross-domain transfer (Wegener/Jobs method)

Pick 3 fields maximally DISTANT from [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal]. For each:
- Strip [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal] of its jargon. Describe it structurally.
- Find a concept from the distant field with matching structure.
- Propose what would change if we imported that concept.
The more distant the field and the tighter the structural match,
the better. Surface analogies are worthless. Find isomorphisms.

Also: what would a smart 12-year-old ask about [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal] that
experts wouldn't? What assumptions are so basic that no paper
bothers to state them?

## AGENT 3: "frame-reverser"
Strategy: Contrarian frame reversal (Kariko/Feynman method)

Take the dominant framing of [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal] and systematically invert it:
- Tool -> Subject, Subject -> Tool
- Noise -> Signal, Signal -> Noise
- Bug -> Feature, Feature -> Bug
- Cause -> Effect, Effect -> Cause
- "Everyone knows X" -> "What if not-X?"
Argue the STRONGEST possible case for each reversal. Which one
has the most unexplored potential? Search for supporting evidence.

Also check for EXAPTATION opportunities: what existing tools,
techniques, or byproducts in [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal] could be repurposed for a
completely different function than intended? (Feathers evolved for
warmth, exapted for flight. CDs made for audio, exapted for data.)

## AGENT 4: "first-principles"
Strategy: Axiom audit + rebuild from scratch (Musk/Feynman method)

1. List the top 5 foundational assumptions in [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal].
2. For each: is this a LAW (physics, logic) or a CONVENTION
   (tradition, habit, historical accident)?
3. Drop all conventions. Keep only laws.
4. Rebuild from scratch: given only the laws and current
   capabilities, what would you design?
5. Where does your design differ from reality? That gap is
   where innovation lives.

## AGENT 5: "constraint-breaker"
Strategy: Constraint injection + eliminative reasoning
(Apollo 13/Semmelweis method)

Two tasks:

TASK A -- FORCED CONSTRAINTS: Solve the core challenge of [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal]
under these artificial constraints:
- You CANNOT use the top 3 standard approaches
- You have 1/10th the typical budget/resources
- It must work for the hardest edge case, not the average case
What solutions emerge when the conventional path is blocked?

TASK B -- ELIMINATIVE REASONING: What are the top 3 competing
explanations or approaches in [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal]? For each pair, design a
test that would ELIMINATE one. Which approaches survive elimination?
What does that reveal?

## AGENT 6: "red-team"
Strategy: Adversarial critique (OpenAI red-team method)

Your job is to DESTROY the conventional wisdom about [accomplishing a goal. By this I mean building complex things. Maybe a skyscrapper, A software, A company: A general way of building things and being able to achieve that really complex goal].
- What is the current "best practice"? Find its fatal flaw.
- For each criticism: is this based on EVIDENCE or CONVENTION?
- What would replace the status quo if it fell?
- What's the strongest argument that the entire field is
  approaching this wrong?
- Distinguish between SOCIAL refutation (experts disagree) and
  EMPIRICAL refutation (evidence contradicts). Only the latter
  should kill an idea.

---

## AFTER ALL AGENTS REPORT -- SYNTHESIS (you, the orchestrator):

1. Collect all findings. Look for CONVERGENCE across agents:
   where do independently-derived insights point to the same thing?
   Weight convergent evidence from independent sources heavily --
   this is how Wegener built the case for continental drift.

2. Look for PRODUCTIVE CONTRADICTIONS between agents:
   where do two agents disagree in a way that suggests a
   higher-order insight? (Janusian thinking: hold both opposites
   simultaneously and search for a framework that contains both.)

3. Apply VERBALIZED SAMPLING to the top candidates:
   For each top insight, estimate how likely a typical domain expert
   would be to suggest it. If >30%, it's too conventional -- push
   further. The best insights live in the <10% zone.

4. Produce a final report:
   - Top 3 novel insights (with convergent evidence from
     multiple agents)
   - For each: the insight, supporting evidence, and a
     concrete experiment to test it
   - Score each on:
     * Novelty (1-10): how far from conventional thinking?
     * Evidence (1-10): how much cross-agent convergence?
     * Testability (1-10): how concrete is the experiment?
     * Distance (1-10): how different from existing literature?
   - One "wild card" -- the single most surprising thing
     any agent found, even if poorly supported

5. Pick the #1 insight and generate an action plan:
   what would you do TOMORROW to start testing it?
```

## Optional: Deep Dive Extension

After the initial run, paste this to go deeper on the best insight:

```
Now pick the highest-scoring insight from the synthesis and do 4 more passes:

1. VERBALIZED SAMPLING: Generate 5 approaches to testing this insight.
   Assign each a probability of how likely a typical expert would suggest it.
   Then generate 3 more with <5% probability -- the unexpected ones.
   Focus on the unexpected ones.

2. EVOLUTIONARY MUTATION: Take the best approach and make one surprising
   change to it. Then another. Then another. Keep the best variant from
   each round. Do 3 rounds.

3. EXAPTATION SCAN: What existing tools, infrastructure, or techniques
   (even from unrelated fields) could be repurposed to test or implement
   this insight? What's already out there that was built for something
   else but has the right structural properties?

4. NOVELTY CHECK: Search the web for the top 10 results related to this
   insight. How is your version DIFFERENT from what already exists? If it's
   not different enough, push further until it is.
```
